## Relevant Industry Standards or Protocols

- **Bluebook Citation Standard** — The dominant US legal citation format; AI tools must produce Bluebook-compliant citations to be trusted by attorneys.
- **ALWD Citation Manual** — Alternative citation standard used by many law schools; relevant for brief drafting accuracy.
- **Legal Information Institute (LII) / Cornell Open Access Standards** — Framework for freely accessible primary legal materials; shapes open-source database design.
- **ABA Model Rule 1.1 (Competence)** — Requires attorneys to understand benefits and risks of technology; drives AI tool adoption governance in firms.
- **OSCOLA (Oxford Standard for Citation of Legal Authorities)** — Dominant citation standard in UK/Commonwealth jurisdictions; relevant for international tools.
- **GDPR / Data Residency Requirements** — Attorney-client privilege and data sovereignty constraints govern where research queries and documents may be processed.

## AI-Native Opportunity

- **Hallucination elimination via grounded retrieval**: The most critical unmet need is zero-hallucination legal citation. Existing tools still produce 17–34% citation errors. An AI-native OSS system built around retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) over verified, version-controlled primary sources — with citation fingerprinting and automatic validation against public court databases — directly addresses the profession's top AI concern.
- **Jurisdiction-aware reasoning**: Current tools treat legal questions generically. An AI that understands the hierarchical authority structure (controlling vs persuasive precedent, circuit splits, state vs federal), and can automatically prioritize sources by jurisdiction relevance and recency, goes beyond keyword search in a way incumbents have not fully solved.
- **Brief drafting with citation provenance**: Attorneys spend hours manually assembling cited arguments into briefs. An AI system that generates draft argument sections with inline, verified citations — traceable to source documents — and exports in Bluebook/ALWD format would compress brief drafting from days to hours.
- **Open database as OSS differentiation**: All major research databases are closed and proprietary. An OSS project built on top of freely available primary law (Public.Resource.Org, CourtListener/RECAP, LII, GPO) could offer a no-cost research layer that covers a large fraction of common legal research needs, particularly for legal aid, law schools, and solo practitioners in underserved markets.
- **Regulatory monitoring agent**: Corporate legal teams need continuous monitoring of regulatory changes across multiple jurisdictions. An AI-native OSS agent that watches Federal Register, state regulatory dockets, and EU Official Journal — summarizes changes, maps them to affected contracts or policies, and generates alerts — addresses a gap that existing point solutions handle poorly.
